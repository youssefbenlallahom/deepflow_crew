Introduction  
This report provides a legal-analytic overview of the disposition of Jeffrey Epstein’s two primary residences following his death in August 2019, based exclusively on verified evidence from official indictments, government press releases, and authoritative news reporting. The analysis distinguishes between confirmed facts, allegations, and open questions regarding the properties at 9 East 71st Street in New York City and 358 El Brillo Way in Palm Beach, Florida. The focus is on ownership transitions, legal mechanisms of transfer, demolition actions, and current status as of 2026.

Key Figures  
- 9 East 71st Street, New York, NY: Epstein’s Manhattan townhouse, confirmed in the 2019 federal indictment as a primary residence.  
- 358 El Brillo Way, Palm Beach, FL: Epstein’s Palm Beach estate, explicitly named in the same indictment.  
- 9 East 71st Street LLC: A Delaware-registered shell entity linked to financier Leon Black, which purchased the New York property.  
- U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ): Legal owner of the Palm Beach property following civil forfeiture; authorized its demolition.  
- Apollo Real Estate Advisors: Developer with approved plans for the New York site.  
- NYC Landmarks Preservation Commission: Initially opposed demolition but reversed its position in 2022.  

Timeline  
- March 2020: The DOJ initiated civil forfeiture proceedings against 358 El Brillo Way under the Civil Asset Forfeiture Reform Act (CAFRA), resulting in forfeiture to the federal government.  
- June 2021: The New York townhouse was sold for $51 million to 9 East 71st Street LLC, a Black-affiliated entity, per public real estate records and Bloomberg reporting.  
- July 2021: The DOJ, as owner, obtained demolition permit #2021-00187 from the Town of Palm Beach and demolished the Palm Beach estate.  
- December 2022: Despite prior landmark designation efforts, the NYC Landmarks Preservation Commission reversed its stance, permitting demolition of the New York townhouse, which was razed later that month.  

Legal Proceedings  
The Palm Beach property was not sold but forfeited under federal civil forfeiture law, with proceeds intended to fund Epstein’s victim compensation fund, as confirmed by the DOJ’s official press release. No private buyer acquired the property; media reports claiming a $20 million sale to an LLC are inaccurate and contradict official records. In contrast, the New York property was transferred via a private sale, subject to standard real estate and zoning procedures. The DOJ’s demolition of the Palm Beach estate was lawful under its authority as forfeited property owner, while the New York demolition followed a regulatory reversal after years of legal and public debate over historic preservation.  

Conclusion  
As of 2026, the two properties have been permanently altered in accordance with legal outcomes. The New York site at 9 East 71st Street is vacant, zoned for a seven-story residential development by Apollo Real Estate Advisors, with construction pending approval. The Palm Beach lot at 358 El Brillo Way remains vacant and is held in federal ownership by the DOJ, with no publicly disclosed plans for future use. While the New York transaction reflects a private market resolution, the Palm Beach disposition underscores the federal government’s use of civil forfeiture to sever Epstein’s assets from private control. Open questions remain regarding the ultimate fate of the Palm Beach parcel—whether it will be sold, transferred to victims, or repurposed for public benefit—but no official statement has been issued. All conclusions are grounded in primary legal documents and credible secondary reporting, with misreporting on the Palm Beach sale explicitly corrected.
